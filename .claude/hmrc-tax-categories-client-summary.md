# DRIVE & HMRC Making Tax Digital — Tax Categories & The Big Choice

**For:** Client review
**Date:** 2026-04-29
**Plain-English summary of how instructors will file Income Tax through DRIVE.**

---

## 1. The picture in one paragraph

From April 2026, sole-trader driving instructors earning over £50,000 must file their Income Tax digitally with HMRC every quarter (then again, as a final declaration, at year-end). DRIVE is being built to do this for them — they enter their lessons and expenses as they always have, and at the end of each quarter DRIVE submits the totals straight to HMRC. To make that work, every category an instructor uses in DRIVE has to be matched to one of HMRC's fifteen approved expense categories. This document covers that matching, plus the single biggest decision the instructor has to make: how to claim for the cost of running their tuition car.

## 2. What instructors enter in DRIVE today

DRIVE already lets instructors record:

**Expenses** (money going out of the business):
- Advertising
- Association memberships (ADINJC, MSA, etc.)
- Bank charges
- Computer / DVSA fees
- Equipment
- Food & drink
- Fuel
- Insurance
- Internet
- MOT

**Payments** (other money out):
- Franchise payouts
- HMRC tax
- Insurance
- Referral commissions
- Pupil-transfer referrals

**Mileage** — every business trip logged with date, start, end, miles.

**Lessons** — every lesson is the source of income (turnover).

## 3. What HMRC accepts

HMRC's Making Tax Digital system only allows expenses to be declared under **fifteen fixed categories**. Every line of expense an instructor records has to fall into one of those fifteen, or be excluded altogether (some things just aren't tax-deductible at all — see "Food & drink" below).

The fifteen HMRC categories are:

| HMRC category | What goes in it |
|---|---|
| Cost of goods | Stock bought for resale (rarely relevant for instructors) |
| Payments to subcontractors | Other instructors paid to cover lessons |
| Wages and staff costs | Employed staff salaries |
| **Car / van / travel expenses** | All vehicle running costs **OR** flat-rate mileage allowance — *the big one for instructors* |
| Premises running costs | Office rent, home-office bills |
| Maintenance costs | Repairs to the premises (not the vehicle) |
| Admin costs | Phone, internet, stationery, software |
| Business entertainment | Not deductible — but reported |
| Advertising | Marketing, lead generation, referral commissions |
| Interest on bank / other loans | Loan interest |
| Finance charges | Bank charges, card processing fees |
| Irrecoverable debts | Bad debts written off |
| Professional fees | Trade body subs, accountant fees, ADI registration |
| Depreciation | Not deductible — but reported |
| Other expenses | Anything that doesn't fit above |

## 4. How DRIVE's categories map to HMRC's

This is the proposed matching. Items shown as "Excluded" are recorded in DRIVE for the instructor's own records but are not sent to HMRC because they aren't tax-deductible.

### Expenses

| DRIVE category | HMRC category | Tax-deductible? |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising | Advertising | ✅ |
| Association memberships | Professional fees | ✅ |
| Bank charges | Finance charges | ✅ |
| Computer / DVSA fees | Professional fees | ✅ |
| Equipment | Other expenses *(or capital allowance if expensive)* | ✅ |
| Food & drink | — | ❌ Recommend removing from DRIVE |
| Fuel | Car / van / travel expenses | ⚠️ Method-dependent — see §5 |
| Vehicle insurance | Car / van / travel expenses | ⚠️ Method-dependent — see §5 |
| Business / public-liability insurance | Other expenses | ✅ |
| Internet | Admin costs | ✅ (business-use proportion only) |
| MOT | Car / van / travel expenses | ⚠️ Method-dependent — see §5 |

### Payments

| DRIVE category | HMRC category | Tax-deductible? |
|---|---|---|
| Franchise payout | Professional fees | ✅ |
| HMRC tax | — | ❌ Income tax is never an allowable expense |
| Insurance | (split — see Expenses table) | ✅ |
| Referral / pupil-transfer referral | Advertising | ✅ |

### Three small changes proposed
1. **Split "Insurance" into two categories** — *Vehicle insurance* and *Business insurance*. They go to different HMRC buckets and need distinguishing.
2. **Remove "Food & drink"** from the picker. Subsistence is only tax-deductible on overnight business trips or temporary workplaces — not relevant to a typical instructor's day. Keeping it on the list misleads users into thinking lunch is claimable.
3. **Consider adding** *Phone* and *Accountant fees* — both common driving-instructor expenses with no current category.

## 5. The big decision: Simplified Expenses vs Actual Costs

This is the single most important decision an instructor will make through DRIVE, and it's not one DRIVE can make for them — it's a tax decision with material consequences.

For the **car / van / travel expenses** category, HMRC offers two completely different methods. Once chosen for a vehicle, the choice is **locked for the lifetime of that vehicle** (they can choose again when they buy a new one). Picking the wrong one can cost the instructor thousands of pounds over the life of the car.

### Method 1 — Simplified Expenses (flat-rate mileage)

**The deal:** HMRC pays a fixed rate per business mile, and that single number covers *every* running cost the car has — fuel, insurance, MOT, servicing, tyres, repairs, road tax, breakdown cover, depreciation. The instructor doesn't add them separately, doesn't keep receipts. Just the miles.

**The rates** (set by HMRC, unchanged since 2011):
- 45p per business mile for the first 10,000 miles each tax year
- 25p per business mile after that

**What's still claimed separately on top:** parking, tolls, congestion charge.

**Day-to-day work for the instructor:**
- Keep a mileage log — date, journey, miles, reason. DRIVE already does this.
- That's it. No fuel receipts. No filing.

**What HMRC needs as evidence if they enquire:**
- The mileage log. End of audit trail.

**Big restriction:** The instructor cannot *also* claim for fuel, vehicle insurance, MOT, servicing, repairs, road tax, breakdown cover, or capital allowances on the car. Pick Simplified, and those receipts are for the instructor's own records only — they don't reduce their tax bill.

### Method 2 — Actual Costs

**The deal:** Add up every real running cost during the year, work out what proportion was business use (typically business miles ÷ total miles), and claim that proportion. Plus the instructor can claim **capital allowances** on the cost of the car itself.

**What gets summed:**
- Fuel
- Vehicle insurance
- MOT
- Servicing & maintenance
- Repairs (including big unexpected ones — gearbox, clutch, etc.)
- Road tax (VED)
- Breakdown cover
- Tyres, wipers, washer fluid
- Lease payments (if leased rather than owned)
- **Plus** capital allowances on the purchase price, which can write off most or all of the cost of a new car in year one (especially generous for electric cars).

**Day-to-day work for the instructor:**
- Keep the mileage log (still needed — it's how the business-use percentage is calculated).
- **Plus** keep receipts and records for every single running cost. Every fuel receipt, the insurance schedule, MOT certificate, every garage invoice, road tax confirmation, breakdown cover renewal.
- Log each one into DRIVE against the right category.

**What HMRC needs as evidence if they enquire:**
- The mileage log
- Every receipt for every cost claimed — six years of retention.

---

### Side-by-side at a glance

|  | Simplified | Actual |
|---|---|---|
| **Day-to-day record-keeping** | Mileage log only | Mileage log + every receipt |
| **Receipts to retain** | None for vehicle costs | All vehicle costs, 6 years |
| **Time cost per quarter** | ~30 min (logging miles) | ~2-4 hours (logging + filing receipts) |
| **HMRC enquiry exposure** | Low — flat rate, hard to argue with | Medium — every line item is a potential question |
| **Captures purchase cost of the car** | ❌ No | ✅ Yes (capital allowances) |
| **Captures big repair year** | ❌ No (instructor eats the cost) | ✅ Yes |
| **Works well with electric cars** | ⚠️ OK but loses ground | ✅ Strong (full first-year allowance + cheap running) |
| **Can switch method later** | ❌ Locked for the life of the vehicle | ❌ Locked for the life of the vehicle |

## 6. Five worked scenarios

All scenarios assume 95% business use (typical for an ADI's tuition car).

### Scenario A — Average full-time instructor, settled mid-life car
*28,000 business miles, 5-year-old hatchback, no major repairs, no purchase that year.*

| Method | Calculation | Deduction |
|---|---|---|
| Simplified | (10,000 × 45p) + (18,000 × 25p) | **£9,000** |
| Actual | £6,135 of running costs × 95% | £5,828 |

**Simplified wins by ~£3,200.** This is the most common scenario for established instructors.

### Scenario B — Part-time instructor, low miles
*10,000 business miles, similar car.*

| Method | Deduction |
|---|---|
| Simplified | **£4,500** (10,000 × 45p) |
| Actual | £3,073 |

**Simplified wins.** Even at low miles, the 45p rate generally beats actual running costs.

### Scenario C — Brand new car bought this year (£22,000)
*28,000 business miles, otherwise as Scenario A.*

| Method | Year-1 deduction |
|---|---|
| Simplified | £9,000 |
| Actual | **£26,728** (£5,828 running costs + £20,900 capital allowance on the car) |

**Actual wins by ~£17,700 — but only in year one.** From year two onwards the capital allowance shrinks dramatically and Simplified usually overtakes again. This is the single biggest reason to consider Actual.

### Scenario D — Major unexpected repair year
*28,000 business miles, gearbox replacement £2,800.*

| Method | Deduction |
|---|---|
| Simplified | £9,000 |
| Actual | £8,488 |

**Roughly even.** Actual catches up but doesn't quite overtake. The instructor would have needed to predict the repair to choose Actual — the lifetime lock means they're stuck with whatever they picked years ago.

### Scenario E — Electric vehicle
*28,000 business miles, EV bought new for £30,000, ultra-low running costs.*

| Method | Year 1 | Year 2+ |
|---|---|---|
| Simplified | £9,000 | £9,000 |
| Actual | **£30,595** (huge first-year EV allowance) | £2,095 |

**Actual wins year 1 massively, Simplified wins every year after.** The EV scenario shows the lock-in trap most starkly. Net over a 7-year ownership, Simplified is usually still better unless the instructor changes car every 3-4 years.

---

## 7. Our recommendation: Simplified, by default

For the **vast majority** of driving instructors, **Simplified is the right answer**. Reasons:

1. **It usually deducts more.** Scenarios A, B, D, and E (years 2+) all favour Simplified. That's the majority of years for the majority of instructors.
2. **It's dramatically less work.** A mileage log instead of six years of receipt-hoarding.
3. **Lower HMRC enquiry exposure.** The flat-rate calculation is hard to challenge.
4. **It's what most accountants suggest** for this profession unless the instructor changes vehicles frequently or has just bought a brand-new (especially electric) car.

The one **clear exception** is the year an instructor buys a new car. If they've spent £15,000–£30,000 on a new tuition vehicle, Actual could deduct £5,000–£25,000 more *in that year*. But they're then locked into Actual for the life of that vehicle, and from year two the maths almost always swings back in Simplified's favour.

## 8. How DRIVE will surface this choice

The proposed instructor experience:

1. **First-time setup.** When the instructor connects DRIVE to HMRC, we ask one question: *"Have you bought a new car for tuition in the current tax year, or do you plan to in the next twelve months?"*
2. **If "No"** → Recommend Simplified. Show a side-by-side comparison panel using the instructor's own historical mileage and expense data so they see the actual numbers, then one-click confirm.
3. **If "Yes"** → Show both options prominently, with year-1 *and* projected-year-2-onwards numbers. Clear note: *"This is a decision worth taking with your accountant if you have one."* No auto-suggestion — they have to actively choose.
4. **Ongoing reminder.** A persistent note on the tax profile: *"This vehicle is on the [Simplified / Actual] method. Locked for the life of this vehicle. When you replace it, you can choose again."*
5. **Year-end "what if" report.** Show the instructor what they'd have deducted on the *other* method this year, so they can make a more informed call when it's time for a new car.

## 9. Three decisions we need from you

These are the decisions only the client can make — they're business calls, not technical ones.

1. **Do most of your instructors change vehicles often (every 2-3 years), or do they keep cars for 5+ years?**
   *If frequent change → Actual is more often attractive (more capital-allowance moments). If long-hold → Simplified almost always wins.*

2. **Should DRIVE explicitly recommend Simplified, or stay neutral and just show the comparison?**
   *Recommending creates a small liability if an instructor would have done better on Actual. Staying neutral is safer but less useful. Our preference is to recommend, with a clear "if you have an accountant, ask them" caveat.*

3. **Once an instructor has submitted their first quarterly return on a chosen method, should DRIVE hard-lock the method, or allow it to be changed with a strong warning?**
   *Hard lock is safer; soft lock with warning is more forgiving of early mistakes. Our preference: soft lock with warning, because DRIVE isn't the source of truth — HMRC is — and instructors may need to correct an early miscategorisation.*

## 10. Two related pieces of work that come along with this

These don't need separate decisions from you — they're consequences of HMRC's record-keeping rules — but worth being aware of so they're costed into the build.

### 10a. Records cannot be deleted once submitted

HMRC requires every record that contributed to a submission to be retained for **6 years** from the end of the relevant tax year. In practice this means:

- A finance row not yet submitted to HMRC → instructor can edit or delete freely.
- A finance row that has been part of a quarterly submission, but the year hasn't been final-declared yet → instructor can correct it (DRIVE will resubmit the amendment to HMRC), but cannot delete it.
- A finance row in a year where the Final Declaration has been filed → frozen. No edits, no deletes, until the 6-year retention expires.

The same applies to the receipt PDF/photo attached to the row. DRIVE will manage this automatically — the delete button simply becomes disabled with a clear explanation when the row is locked. No instructor action needed.

### 10b. Year-end download archive

Every instructor will want, at year-end (and any time their accountant asks), a complete archive of their tax-year records. Proposed feature:

- **One-click "Download tax year archive"** button on the HMRC tab for any completed tax year.
- Generates a ZIP file containing:
  - All finance rows for the year as a spreadsheet
  - All mileage logs as a spreadsheet
  - All receipts (PDFs and photos) filed by quarter
  - Each HMRC submission (the data sent + HMRC's response + correlation ID)
  - A human-readable summary PDF cover sheet
- Sent to the instructor by email when ready (large archives may take a minute or two).
- Used for routine accountant handover at year-end *and* as an HMRC enquiry response pack on the rare occasions HMRC asks.

Multi-year archives and custom date ranges aren't planned for v1 — single-tax-year is enough to cover the vast majority of needs.
